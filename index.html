Workaway - Sicily

Part Sixteen - Concerted? A Little

Saturday, 8th February 13:35 CET

We continued our efforts in restoring the furniture that’s building up in the church. I’m doing the menial jobs such as sanding and varnishing. Repeating the same continuous motion becomes relaxing after a while. Almost tranquil and peaceful. Then it had to start raining so the second half of my work detail continued indoors. I’ve been reorganising Silvio’s Shakespeare folios into comedies, tragedies and histories. They’re in Italian but I can deduce which play is which by the title and picture on the front.

Bet you didn’t know that Shakespeare was merely a public figurehead, while his secret lover—conveniently erased from history—was the true genius behind the plays. At least, that’s the latest gem Silvio has decided to enlighten me with. Now, it’s hardly scandalous to suggest that Shakespeare may have been gay, but to reduce him to some illiterate puppet, a glorified Elizabethan influencer? The sheer ignominy of it! My innate British heritage is screaming; I think I’ve given myself an ulcer from biting my tongue so frequently. How dare he disparage the Bard with such unqualified drivel?

Sally walks in, interrupting Silvio’s conspiracy lessons, desperation in his voice.

He explains, “I’ve been seen”.

A newcomer in the neighbourhood, carrying an expensive-looking camera—an unmistakable red flag. I suppose it was that two-and-a-half hour walk from Tomasso Natale to Palermo centre that put me on the radar. Walking through every neighbourhood, snapping away. I guess that was asinine given the dodgy areas I was wandering through.

So, in no uncertain terms, I’m ordered not to leave the compound; get Silvio to drive me to the station and text whenever I return so that either he or Sally can collect me. And if I dare to wander around Tommaso Natale, I must ensure it’s glaringly obvious that I’m not carrying a backpack.

Silvio, of course, waves off the warning with his usual insouciance. The world isn’t dangerous, remember? Perhaps to him, with his boundless optimism and questionable past, but I’ve always been one to err on the side of caution. Do I believe Sally’s concerns? I’m unsure. Part of me thinks it’s all performative theatrics, a melodrama put on for the benefit of an impressionable outsider. Yet, another part—one that leans towards self-preservation—knows better than to dismiss the unease outright.

Maybe I need something concrete, a sign that I’m truly in the crosshairs. A Memento Mori delivered to my doorstep, a Sicilian calling card that screams: You should have listened.

Part Fifteen - The Man Beneath the Façade

Friday, 7th February 22:11 CET

Silvio is NUTS. Like, genuinely, certifiably nuts. I had my suspicions early on, little clues dropped in past conversations, but it’s taken me a while to fully piece together just how deep this madness runs. He’s like Dominic, but with a more unhinged collection of conspiracy theories rattling around in his brain.

Before COVID, he was a music teacher. That is, until he refused to get the vaccine, putting both students and staff at risk. Not content with just being a personal health hazard, he took it upon himself to start proselytising, urging others to resist the jab as well. Naturally, he got sacked. Rather than accept that actions have consequences, he doubled down, convinced that he was the last bastion of truth in a world brainwashed by “Big Pharma” and government control.

After that, his response was to open his home to complete strangers, all to prove that the world isn’t a big and scary place. I, for one, completely disagree. The world is a big and scary place, and people like Silvio are a prime example of why. I’m not saying don’t explore; I’m saying remain vigilant and assess potential risks. But, of course, arguing with him would be like reasoning with a lunatic, so I nod along and let him ramble.

Then there’s his newfound adoration for Communism, which, hilariously, seems to have stemmed from a single Workaway volunteer from Poland. This guy lived under Communist rule and, apparently, now makes less money in a democratic society than he did under an authoritarian regime. Instead of recognising that as anecdotal at best, Silvio has taken this as indisputable proof that communism is the superior way of life. Never mind the historical evidence, the oppression, or the fact that people literally risked their lives escaping it—no, one broke Polish bloke is all the confirmation Silvio needs.

And then we get to the real kicker: his entire living situation is dodgy as hell. He built this house from the ground up not out of some noble, off-the-grid, self-sufficient dream, but because he was banned from owning property after running an illegal hostel at his previous residence. Again, this isn’t speculation—this is coming directly from him, said with the casual air of someone discussing the weather. He wasn’t confessing, he was bragging.

But the cherry on top? The church. The derelict church he’s so lovingly restoring isn’t just some quaint pet project or an attempt to give back to the community. No, no, no. He’s renovating it because his son is gay, and he genuinely believes this will somehow “bring him back to the light.” I mean, is it just a church, or is Silvio planning something more grandiose, some twisted little conversion therapy cult under the guise of religious salvation? At this point, I wouldn’t put anything past him.

It’s actually mental. The more I learn, the more I’m grateful I’m only here for the week instead of a month.

Part Fourteen - Palmero = Done ✅

Friday, 7th February 19:02 CET

Silvio and I decided to take another day off since the weather was perfect. After he prepared me yet another sublime sandwich, I set off for Palermo for the fifth day in a row. But today was different—I walked the entire way. Two and a half hours, passing every station along the train line.

Once I reached the city centre, I didn’t stop there. I continued south, hugging the coastline, hoping to stumble upon some natural beauty—golden sands, crystal-clear waters, something to redeem Palermo’s rugged exterior. But as I walked further, it became clear that if I kept going in search of paradise, I might as well circumnavigate the entire island. Eventually, I turned back, accepting that Palermo is not quite the grand holiday destination I had imagined. I spent the rest of the afternoon reading Orson Welles’ biography in the park.

After five days of exploring and covering nearly nine miles a day, I feel I know this city like the back of my hand. Each quarter has its own distinct character. The northeast is picturesque, tourist-friendly, and home to the harbour. The northwest blends verdant parks and grand cathedrals with creeping signs of poverty. The southeast is a grittier stretch—mainly an exit road flanked by a bleak, debris-strewn coastline. The southwest, meanwhile, could easily be mistaken for a slum in the Middle East.

Still, I’ve seen it all.

Part Thirteen - Screams, Scams and Skinny Jeans

Thursday, 6th February 23:15 CET

My first real-world experience with the perfidy of the Sicilians happened tonight. I’d heard the stories about the Mafia and witnessed firsthand the poor conditions they impose on the capital, but a part of me still found it hard to believe the veracity of the tales I’d been told.

Let me start from the beginning. After a very filling tea consisting of - you guessed it, dough, potatoes and ham in a new combination - Sally and I drove to pub around fifteen minutes away from Silvio’s place to watch the football. He’s still slightly depressed after his beloved Arsenal were humiliated last night. He wanted to see Spurs receive the same treatment but that didn’t come to fruition.

As mentioned, he is Sri Lankan but has lived here in Sicily for over a decade working as a handyman. He came over on a cricket scholarship and never left, very much suiting the congruous criminality of Tomasso Natale.

The pub wasn’t in Palermo but in a place called Vespi. It’s authentically Sicilian. Tourists don’t come this far out of the city - for safety reasons. Everyone’s drinking scotch, smoking cigars. I can’t even see the ceiling because of the thick layer of smoke. We were at the bar enjoying the game, staying out of trouble, until half time at least.

Then we hear a scream from outside. The only reason I mention it is because of the slightly obscure reaction of everyone in the bar. The reaction was no reaction. It sounded different from a girl just falling over. To me, it sounded more like a shriek of pain but I am just speculating.

I instinctively get off my bar stool to go look at the commotion, but my arm’s seized by Sally who hasn’t moved. I’m trying to remember but I don’t think anyone was too perturbed by the scream. It was definitely loud enough that everyone could hear. I get back on my stool and ask Sally what’s going on. He’s swirling his scotch around the glass and seems to be lost in a reverie.

The game finishes and Sally’s chatting to friends at the bar. This is one of the only sports bars outside of Palermo that’s not Mafia controlled - apparently – which is why we’re here. I’m still not entirely sure what that actually means. Are Mafia bars like fronts for laundering money? Are they storing stolen goods? Or just skimming a cut of the profits under the guise of “protection” fees? Of course, all my conclusions are based on A Bronx Tale, Goodfellas, and Donnie Brasco.

I’m waiting for Sally outside for a few minutes. It’s a convivial atmosphere. It’s a young crowd. My sort of age. Out of nowhere, an Italian bird comes up to me and starts a conversation. I don’t speak Italian, and she doesn’t speak English, though I do speak a little Spanish. Va va vroom.

With me being a cheeky-chappie, and my “everyone loves an Englishman” attitude, we’re fumbling through this conversation. Her attempts to bridge the language barrier with her gestures and eager smiles are more endearing than effective, but we’re getting somewhere.

She’s clearly growing impatient, though. With a flirtatious twirl of her hand, she leans in closer. Before I even have time to register what’s happening, she swings both arms around me and is kissing me. At first, I’m caught off guard, thinking this is just the Italian way—passionate, spontaneous, a trademark of Sicilian culture. It’s all part of the dance, right? The grand romantic gesture, the universal language of love that everyone talks about when describing the Italian spirit.

Just as I’m patting my ego on the back - her hands move lower. I feel a sudden, unexpected tug at the pocket of my jeans. The subtle motion is so fluid, it could have been mistaken for just another affectionate touch—if I hadn’t felt the unmistakable shift in weight as she tried to lift my phone from my pocket.

I’m being robbed.

It was an odd feeling. For a second, I’m frozen. My brain is registering what’s happening, but my body lags behind, unsure. As soon as I felt significant weight shift, I think instinct kicked in.

Thank God for my skinny jeans. I’ve never been so grateful for tight pockets in my life. With a quick movement, I catch my phone, her supple fingers let go and I’m just looking her directly in the eyes.

This creeped me out. I’ve never seen anyone’s eyes change so vehemently before. Especially someone with such beautiful baby blues… She went from Holly Golightly to Baby Jane real fast. And we all know how terrified I am of the latter.

Before anything is said, Sally’s once again grabbing me by the arm leading me toward the car. Now I get it. The romance, the flirting? All part of the game. It’s not my charm she’s interested in—just what’s in my trousers. My phone, I mean. My phone.

I was experiencing a bit of a head rush as we were driving off. I semi-knew what happened but I was still fuzzy. In the car, Sally’s letting me in on the street scams. “They work in teams,” he said, “and if they know you’ve got expensive gear, they’ll target you again.” Well, great to know, and here I thought she was just entranced by my English charm. I’ll have to remember this scam for when the Parisian lucky lucky men come my way.

I’m still confused about the scream though. He didn’t answer that question, but I doubt it’s as sinister as what we’re all thinking. Sicily may not be as progressive as I had thought but it’s not ran by gangsters and the Mafia in the way that the movies will have us believe. Or am I still being incredibly naïve?

Part Twelve - Palermo at My Own Pace

Thursday, 6th February 18:50 CET

My first day off, and after my little tribulation last night, I needed it. The sun was out, the tasks weren’t urgent, and Silvio let me have the full day to myself—on the condition that I’d work one day over the weekend. No complaints from me, since the weather’s set to turn miserable by Saturday night anyway.

So, back to Palermo. I tried to catch a walking tour at 11 AM, only to find they don’t exist. Just the 10 AM ones, which was annoying. But since I’ve already combed through this city—top to bottom, east to west, seeing both the picturesque and the profane, the sublime among the malign—today was a much more relaxed affair. Stuck to the high street, indulged in a little window shopping, sat down in every park I passed, and gave my feet the break they desperately deserved.

For lunch, Silvio had made me a sandwich. Not very Italian I know, but regardless, it was one of the most delicious sandwiches I’ve ever had in my life. It puts my turkey and cheese ensemble to shame. It was the size of a small pancake, filled with fried chicken, lettuce, mozzarella, mayonnaise, zucchini, aubergine, a rare herb found only in the Himalayas, the blood of a mountain goat, the vestiges of the grain forged in the heart of Mount Vesuvius. You get the picture.

After finishing one fiction yesterday, I started the Orson Welles biography right in front of the Norman Palace. From the first few chapters, he sounds like a relative of Jay Gatsby, interacting with artists, artisans, poets, impresarios, screenwriters, authors, and even Moroccan royalty, all before the youthful crescent of 20! He is unequivocally a gifted child, processing the elocution of a actor much older, becoming a Shakespeare afficionado at twelve, writing his first screenplay at sixteen, Broadway at seventeen. You read about people like this and it’s almost they are destined for greatness.

One little blunder this afternoon—the public toilets. First off, they’re gender-neutral. How modern. A neat row of cubicles, surprisingly clean, none of the nightmarish filth you brace yourself for when thinking of public loos. My mistake? I thought they were free.

I go to unlock the door. It doesn’t budge. No problem, probably just jammed. I give it a nudge. Nothing. So, naturally, I resort to shouldering it a few times. It moves slightly, but not enough. One final push—I put my foot through it. Snap. Lock broken. Bit stiff, that, I thought. Of course, it wasn’t stuck. It was locked. Because, as I discovered moments later, Palermo operates on a pay-to-pee system.

Bloody Mafia, taxing us for everything…

I clock my faux pas as I leave and finally spot the euro signs on the door. Good thing there aren’t cameras in there, or I might be expecting a visit from Tommy-Two-Fingers & Jimmy-the-snitch.

Caught the train back to Cardillo just as the first droplets are starting to fall. If I board five minutes before departure, I can usually circumvent the ticket inspectors. The fare’s only €2, but for all I know, the trains are Mafia-run too. And honestly, I don’t need to add another moral failing to my growing list of peccadillos.

Part Eleven - Hallucinogens in the Pasta?

Thursday, 6th February 08:53 CET

I woke up in the middle of the night completely disoriented. No idea if I had a momentary sleepwalk or I rolled the wrong way out of bed, but when I finally came to, I was in the middle of my room, wrapped in my duvet, cowering in a heap on the cold stone floor.

Now, my room doesn’t have any windows. No charging lights. No alarm clock glow. Just thick, suffocating darkness. I tried fumbling for something—anything—to ground myself, but it was pointless. Sat there waiting for my eyes to adjust, but as I’d already discovered on my first morning here, there needs to be a light source for that to happen. No light source, no vision. Which is why, at 10:30 AM, this room is still black as the void.

So I stand up trying to get my balance but the stone floor is freezing my feet. Taking my queen-sized duvet, I’m trying to shuffle to something I can latch onto. I don’t want to be dramatic here, but I do remember my sleep depraved mind thinking what if I’ve gone blind – that’s why I can’t see. I don’t know how long standing, trying to understand what was happening. That’s when I heard a object drop onto the stone slabs.

I drop to my knees, patting the floor trying to find the object; exactly how Velma looked when she dropped her glasses in Scooby-Doo. I found it, it was my laptop mouse. My saving grace in my time of need. The LED light on the underside, the signal guiding me out of my blind purgatory. Clicking the button so the light flashes, it guided me the two feet or so back to my bed. Now, I know this makes me sound completely and utterly retarded, or a dunce, but believe me, it was the most terrified I’ve been in a while thinking I must have gone blind.

As you can imagine, it took me a while to get back off to sleep after that little charade.

Part Ten - Tea Time

Wednesday, 5th February 21:20 CET

Another riveting dinner table conversation with Silvio this evening. Somehow, we (or rather he) ended up on the topic of gender and sexuality. Latest revelation: he’s a right-wing libertarian. Oh, and homophobic. Loves the idea of living off the land, free from government interference. Which, in hindsight, makes total sense—he built this entire bungalow himself. Something tells me there wasn’t much in the way of planning permission involved, but that’s hardly my problem. Very strong opinions on same-sex marriage. Found myself nodding obsequiously just to stop myself being kicked out onto the streets.

The real kicker, though, was his deep dive into what is and isn’t controlled. According to him, every restaurant, every independent supermarket, every souvenir shop in Palermo—all of them answer to someone. Turns out, the reason he’s such a dedicated chef isn’t just a love for cooking. He refuses to give a cent to the local supermarkets because, you guessed it, they’re mob-controlled. Even the tacky little souvenir shops? Yep. I’ve unknowingly contributed to the cause. My €1 magnet is now a permanent stain on my conscience.

Food-wise, tonight was a first—pasta. The trademark of Sicilian gastronomy: Macaroni drowned in cheese with meat, peas, and aubergines. Every single ingredient, on its own? Great. But somehow, the combination was deeply unsettling. It tasted like… sick. Weird huh. Obviously, I kept that little critique to myself. Silvio did, however, comment on my portion sizes—apparently, I eat less than any of his previous fourteen guests.

The routine is simple: at 2 PM, after work, he makes me something—spaghetti, an omelette, or some variation of dough, ham, and cheese. Then I spend my afternoon in Palermo, and by the time I get back at 8 PM, there’s a massive portion of a traditional meal waiting. It’s always still in the pan, ready to be dished up at will. The problem? I’m still full from whatever he made me earlier. And yet, somehow, I still manage to walk away feeling guilty for not eating more.

Part Nine - A Moment of Tranquillity

Wednesday, 5th February 19:13 CET

Since my hamstrings were aching and my feet cramping up, I didn’t do my marathon long walk around Palermo today. After the disappointing display yesterday, I wanted to stick to the more touristy parts. Plus, today was the first day of sun we’ve had that haven’t been accompanied with clouds, so I didn’t need to do any fancy camera trickery to make my pictures look brighter than it actually was. I stuck by the harbour and settled down to read my book. Nice and relaxing after my onerous morning. I spoke to some of the locals in shops and got my customary magnet. Oddly, I’ve had more conversations in Spanish in Sicily than Italian. I’ve found communicating with locals is easier in Spanish as the Italian equivalent is a closer resemblance than English.

A calming day despite the hectic start.

Part Eight - Scrapes & Bruises

Wednesday, 5th February 14:05 CET

I got into a car accident this morning. Well, I say accident… Let me clear a few things up. First of all, I wasn’t driving this time which is a nice change in these situations. No one was hurt, just a bit of cosmetic damage to cars that are little more than scrap value anyway. Now, it was hardly surprising that I witnessed one eventually, because the driving I’ve witnessed is mind-boggling. There are road markings, but they are completely disregarded. In the UK, the responsibility is on the driver at the junction to pull out when safe to do so. Slightly different philosophy here; junctions are futile, and cars don’t stop. The onus is on the driver already speeding at 50kph to either slam on the brakes or embrace their fate. Apparently, playing bumper cars isn’t just the accepted status quo—it’s not even considered a transgression.

Silvio proved this firsthand when he pulled straight onto a roundabout and we got clipped. He didn’t even bother exchanging details with the other driver. “These things happen Harry”. Do they? Because where I’m from, this is what insurance fraud is made of. I had a hint of Silvio’s driving going to Montello beach, zigzagging, driving in the middle of the road, going through red lights, etc. I thought Italy was more evolved than this.

The second half of my workday took a nostalgic turn—right back to my ten-year-old self. Climbing trees and falling out of them.

I was picking oranges, and since the best ones were at the top, I did what any self-respecting monkey would do: scaled the tree. Agile. Efficient. Until my hamstrings decided otherwise.

After days of walking five miles in flat, unsupportive shoes, my feet chose this exact moment to cramp up. Yelping, I took the express route down—flat on my back. The only witness to my humiliation? Chico, the ever-loyal dog, who bolted over and covered me in concerned, slobbery reassurance.

At least someone cared.

Part Seven - The Real Picture

Tuesday, 4th February 19:05 CET

I set my alarm this morning, so I didn’t take the same liberties as yesterday. Though this wasn’t needed because my wall borders the main road, and the council started digging it up to fix the sewage line to the property at seven. Still, I didn’t surface until well after nine.

I was in the shower when I heard the side door squeak and Silvio shout something through to me. Over the noise of the water, I only caught fragments: “Harry, don’t use … … it’s connected to the sewer.”

Have I just washed my hair in sewage water? I’d rather not think about it. I used shampoo, so I’m banking on that being an effective barrier against whatever bacteria was swirling around in there. That’s how shampoo works, right?

The morning’s work was different from yesterday. We were in the church around the side of the property, restoring furniture and parts of the altar. The construction is still in its early stages, so today was mostly sanding. I met Sally, a Sri Lankan handyman hired to maintain the property. And in case there’s any confusion—Sally is a bloke, with a heart of gold and an adorable dog called Chico. He gave me some solid recommendations on places to check out in Palermo and, more importantly, places to avoid.

So, naturally, in true adventurer fashion, I ignored his advice entirely.

When I got to Palermo, I headed west—away from the tourist hubs, away from the postcard version of the city. Big mistake. Three hours of wandering, and what did I see? Bike thieves swiping two phones in broad daylight, a dead dog rotting by the side of the road, kids who couldn’t have been older than twelve smoking weed, and street vendors casually doing a line of something definitely not legal. This was Sicily without the filter. The kind you don’t find in the brochures.

I’ve never wanted to get back to civilised society so badly. I didn’t dare get my camera out for fear of it getting taxed…

I returned to Silvio’s place disconsolate. This isn’t what I had in mind when I wanted to spend the week in Sicily. To make up for this failed expedition, Silvio had an authentic spaghetti recipe waiting for me. It wasn’t in a bolognaise but just in a sauce that was delicious. When I asked for it, you would have thought that I had just harvested his Abuela for organs. “No, no, no, Harry. Not something I can do!”

Family recipes in Sicily aren’t just food; they’re legacy. To be enjoyed, never altered, and certainly never shared. Lesson learned.

Slight digression back to the civilised world of Leicester. As I’m writing this, I’m also in contact with the writers, sorting out the next edition of Demon Magazine. And I’m absolutely howling. Why? Because I’ve just thrown two lads under the bus, pissed off all the women in the group chat with a hilarious (but apparently sexist) gif, and now I’m being threatened with being reported to the student union. (For context, the student union is run by one guy called Dave—quite possibly the most laid-back man alive. The fact that he has full control over every society at the university while maintaining such extreme levels of nonchalance is, frankly, mind-blowing.)

Anyway, let me explain—once I’ve stopped giggling.

So, these two lads both wanted to do a Champions League roundup for the issue. I chimed in with, “If one of you wants to do an article explaining the new CL format, that would be fucking helpful 🤣.” Jess—our ever-efficient editor—promptly assigned both their names to the spreadsheet, effectively sealing their fate. After a bit of back and forth in the chat, they begrudgingly decided who was writing what. That should’ve been the end of it.

But, of course, I couldn’t leave it there.

I suggested giving them both a double-page spread, because, well, I’m a nob. Jess, again, ratified it without hesitation. Hahaha. I threw in a final “Well done, lads—can’t wait to read them!” just to show how gracious I was.

Then Molly, our design editor, piped up. “I don’t even know what CL is.” Instead of simply explaining it, I dropped a Ricky Gervais meme (as shown below). The lads responded with laughing emojis. The women in the chat? A deluge of outrage. I’m bad laughing…

Any who, that’s enough interlocution for this evening.

Part Six - A Day in the Life

Monday, 3rd February 22:19 CET

Woke up at 10:40 this morning—just to set a precedent. Two texts from Silvio instructing me to meet him at the church. Within this small cluster of houses, there’s a church, a few storage buildings, and other assorted structures that seem to serve no clear purpose.

Monday’s tasks were as uninspiring as expected—hauling wood outside the bungalow, sweeping up, tidying the walkways. Menial but necessary. Silvio’s little house, a product of his own hands (and a few Workaway volunteers over the years), stands as a testament to determination—if not necessarily to craftsmanship. For that reason, I can excuse the loose wiring and the temperamental water flow. Consider them charming quirks rather than flaws.

With the jobs done by half one, I headed into Palermo. It felt like Stockholm again—Kindle in hand, zoning out on the metro, heading straight for the centre. The closer we got, the more the scenery improved. Gone were the rundown streets and battered cars. Instead, grand old buildings, buzzing cafés, and streets that actually looked like they belonged in Italy. This was the Palermo I had imagined.

I walked from the station to the docks, covering the harbour and following the shoreline. No real plan—just wandering. The sea breeze, the smell of salt and espresso mixing in the air, the city shifting from chaotic to calm the further I went. The place has layers. Tourists snapping pics outside cathedrals, locals arguing on street corners, old men in sharp suits sipping espresso like they own the place. Maybe they do.

I kept walking until the sun dipped and the streetlights flickered on. That was my cue to turn back, retracing my steps to the station, ready to catch the train home. A solid first look at Palermo. Tomorrow, I go deeper.

25,000 steps and seven miles later, I’m back at Silvio’s with a feast of sausages, roast potatoes and onions waiting for me. Thank goodness it wasn’t more dough. I needed to discreetly bin the leftover pizza that he made me for lunch because the dough’s texture was similar to glue.

Over dinner, Silvio went further into his philosophy of parenting, love of food, staying connected with our roots. Then he moved onto some contentious issues: Palestine-Gaza, Trump, thoughts on homosexuality. Some opinions differed to my own, though I wasn’t going to disagree. Don’t bite the hand that feeds you.

Part Five - First Impressions

Sunday, 2nd February 21:45 CET

Everything I eat this week is going to be some combination of dough and mozzarella. My cholesterol is about to skyrocket. The two meals I’ve had today were pleasant enough, but overwhelmingly dough-based, and I’m already craving some variety. Slim chance. My portion sizes are already small—famously so—but I don’t want to seem impertinent by leaving food unfinished. Hence why I’m sitting here writing, trying to distract myself from the nausea, instead of catching up on much-needed sleep. I was up at 4 a.m. to catch the bus from Mile End—it’s a miracle I’m still awake.

First impressions? Mixed. The house was entirely built by Silvio and a rotating cast of Workaway volunteers, so if a joist collapses in the middle of the night, I won’t be surprised. The area leaves much to be desired, but for just €2 a day, I’ll be escaping to Palermo whenever I can.

Silvio is amicable and always up for conversation. The reason he hosts volunteers, he says, is because his ex-wife has instilled a fear of strangers in their children—taught them to stay away, not to explore, not to trust. Silvio is determined to prove her wrong by inviting strangers into his home. An interesting strategy for winning an argument, especially with someone he openly despises, but hey-ho.

One thing I’ve quickly picked up on in Tommaso Natale—there’s no middle ground when it comes to cars. You’re either driving a battered Toyota or Citroën, held together by sheer willpower, or you’re gliding around in a pristine, brand-new Alfa Romeo or Mercedes. No in-between. The disparity was even more glaring this afternoon at Montello Beach. There are just some cars that no one dared to touch.

Part Four - Roaming the Area

Sunday, 2nd February 17:49 CET

I set off with my camera to explore the neighbourhood, only to find more of the same desolation I had witnessed on the drive in. Litter blanketed the streets, shattered glass crunched underfoot, and every other window seemed to have surrendered to vandalism. The roads were a free-for-all—cars careening past at reckless speeds, emboldened by the absence of cameras or speed bumps. If I were documenting the decay of civilisation, this would be an excellent place to start. They leaned into this tension hard. There were scenes showing protests and vandalism against Shylock’s home and possessions (not in the original text) and some added lines that highlighted the casual antisemitism of the time. It made the stakes feel a lot higher.

Then, after the usual ending, there was a twist. Oberman delivered a monologue about her great-grandmother, who fought the fascists when they tried to evict Jews from London neighborhoods in 1936. It was clearly aimed at the audience, urging kindness and acceptance of different cultures. It felt a bit tacked on—like an addendum to the play—but at least they kept it to the end instead of working it into the story itself.

The Merchant of Venice has always been one of my favorites because of how strong the story is. This version was wildly different but still stayed true to the main themes. It’s definitely sticking with me. That said, I couldn’t figure out who I was supposed to root for: the Jewish moneylender who’s out for literal blood, or the fascists who manipulate her and walk away unscathed. It’s messy, but maybe that’s the point.

Next up: A Midsummer Night’s Dream. But before we get into the magic of the play, let me just say—the Barbican is a maze. The neighborhood is pristine, upper-class, and charming but not in a presumptuous way. But for a tourist? Not exactly user-friendly. I spent a good half hour wandering around before finally tracking down the theatre.

Now, onto the show: It was fantastic. There’s a reason why Midsummer is one of Shakespeare’s greatest hits. The themes are straightforward, which makes it the perfect playground for different interpretations. This production ran three hours but felt like no time at all.

Quick digression: the Barbican Theatre itself? Weird setup. The tiers are super narrow - two or three rows max - but they wrap all the way around to the stage corners. It’s like the Globe, but without the charm. I was up in the fourth tier, and the view made me feel a little dizzy, peering over the edge of the balcony. By the second half, I’d had enough and made a bold move. I shuffled my way down to the first-row stalls. Yep, the £270 seats. Claimed them like I belonged there all along. Do anything with confidence and people will not question it. And honestly? Worth it. I was so close I could feel the actors’ presence—literally. The swish of Lysander’s cloak brushed past me, and I could practically hear the creak of Demetrius’ boots as he stomped across the stage. I even caught the subtle twitch of Mathew Baynton’s eyelid while he was playing dead. Forget immersive theatre—this was Shakespeare in high definition.

This production of Midsummer was pure joy from start to finish. The boys get the girls, they have a dance, the King gives his blessing for his daughter to marry the man she loves and they all live happily ever after. Shakespeare in a nutshell. Puck wrapped it up with a perfect poetic soliloquy, leaving us with smiles and a renewed belief in the magic of live theatre.

Out of all the trips I’ve made to London over the past few months, these shows rank near the top.

Tuesday, 8th October

Let me tell you a story. It’s about a man; tall, debonair, a true gentleman with a penchant for fine things. He carried himself with the grace of nobility, believing firmly that a man’s mood and fortune were shaped, in no small part, by how he presented himself to the world. His garments, he believed, were a reflection of his spirit, and so he took great care to adorn himself in the finest cloth that gold could buy.

This man, though refined and prudent in most affairs, was not immune to the whims of vanity, and the allure of luxury tugged often at his heart. One evening, word reached him of a rare event—a grand market, closed to the commoners, where only those of youthful vigour and standing might enter. It was whispered that the best merchants of the realm would offer their goods at prices so low, they defied reason. A nobleman's wardrobe for a tradesman’s coin.

To make the market more tempting for these young souls, the guilds set forth amusements to capture their fancies. There was music, lively as a royal ball, played by minstrels who stood high on a dais in the midst of the square. Brightly coloured pennants flapped in the evening air, strewn across the market stalls like the banners of a noble tournament. It was like a merry festival, yet strange, for no guiding hand of elder or master was there to rein in the wild spirit of youth. For but two hours, these apprentices had free reign to barter, trade, and snatch up the finest wares at unheard-of discounts, as if time itself had been turned to favour them.

Into this revelry strode our protagonist; a man no different from his peers, eyes wide at the splendour and goods displayed before him. He had no thought of thrift or restraint, for such a night comes but seldom, and never again might such bargains be offered. So, as though possessed by a strange frenzy, he dashed from one stall to the next, his purse jingling with the coin he had saved.

He purchased fine woollen breeches, velvet doublets fit for a nobleman, a grand leather jerkin, and even a hooded cloak emblazoned with the crest of a famous knightly order. Each piece he bought with little thought, for the prices, they said, were far lower than any would find on a common market day. And so, laden with goods, he strode back to his quarters, a triumphant smile upon his face.

Yet, as the moon rose higher in the sky, and the frenzy of the fair faded, the young man sat amidst his newly acquired riches and began to reckon his coin. He gazed in his reflection; in awe at his sartorial elegance for it was then that a troubling thought took root. He had spent far more than he had ever intended. What use had he for so many fine garments? Could he truly afford to keep all that he had purchased? And indeed, did he even need such luxuries, when simpler wares might serve him just as well?

And thus, my friends, this tale stands as a caution to all. For even the sharpest mind can be dulled by the glitter of a fleeting bargain, and in the rush to seize every treasure, one may return home burdened with far more than they need—or can pay for. Better to be the one who walks the fair with a discerning eye and a steady hand, rather than the one who staggers away with empty pockets and full regrets. After all, it’s not the abundance of fine things that brings satisfaction, but the wisdom to leave behind what you never truly needed.

Hopefully, you can deduce the allegory behind that story. I’m sure the more perceptive folk will pick up on the deeper meaning. Or perhaps, much like the man in the story, you’ve been swept up in my fine words and now find yourself sitting there wondering, "Wait… what was the point again?" Most of the societies have their first meetings or activities this week, so my evenings are almost all booked up anyway.

This evening, for instance, I was running with the group. We were doing laps around a track. Lauren is the pace setter, as she can run a mile in around 6.30 minutes. In total, we covered an accumulated 5k, which is a nice round number. Upon finally running in my new trainers, I have come to the conclusion that they are actually half a size too big for me. If I am duplicitous enough, I will clean them up and try to return them. Though that might be too devious even for a man with my flexible moral compass.

After running, I had Pilates booked at the Watershed. I hadn’t eaten anything in five hours, my muscles ached, my groin was tight, and my thigh was on the verge of being pulled. I was satisfied with the exercise I had done for the day and was ready to shower and crawl into bed, but Kyra, one of the runners, persuaded me to attend since she didn’t want to go alone. Having completed a couple of yoga sessions, I was expecting much of the same. In truth, there were a lot of similar moves; however, Pilates focused more on core exercises. Instead of completing them until you hit your rep target, the purpose is to control each movement and take your time, many of which activated my abs more than any workout session in the past. Despite my trepidation, it felt like it soothed my aches and pains; though now, I have the added addition of stinging abs along with painful legs when I wake tomorrow.

Sunday, 29th September

Treachery! I have just found out that ‘Fear Her’ has been removed from BBC iPlayer because that episode contains scenes with the disgraced Hugh Edwards. Doctor Who is well-known for including real newsreaders or presenters to increase the verisimilitude and Britishness of the show. While this contributes to its charm, it also means the show is subject to the ever-shifting landscape of BBC politics, which the corporation must carefully navigate. Since Edwards' declasse status, the Beeb has rightly cut ties with him, but that means that any programs he has appeared in need to be removed from the public domain. Alas, even episodes where he only features for a meagre few seconds. You would think the higher-ups at the Beeb would tactfully dub the scenes or even recut the episodes given the popularity of the show, but no. Hitherto, season two of Doctor Who is missing a much-loved episode…

Moving onto a less contentious part of my day, after last night’s festivities, I am walking around zombie-like today. Anything less than the required eight hours of sleep gives me headaches. Nevertheless, I trekked to Abbey Park to partake in the final fresher event and to round off a very successful week of acclimatising to life back in Leicester. ‘Park Life’, as it is advertised, was supposed to be a carnival-type affair with games, activities, crafts, and sports—mediums to help the new intake mix and socialise. Arriving at midday, an hour into these events, I was greeted with a rather lacklustre show of DSU ambassadors and a scarcely filled park. Only a small group of students decided to show up, meaning the overall success of the event was wavering. It’s one of those things which takes mass participation for the event to be successful. Despite the event being ‘sold out’ on the DSU website, the actual turnout was met with general apathy, so I made a premature exit and had my first relaxing day to myself, where I could watch the football and catch up on my reading.

In other news, Natalia has booked the tickets to see Jimmy Carr after consulting Mehmet. I have been watching this venue for a while, so it’s good to finally have it booked. Alas, now I just need to try and fit it into my busy schedule.

Saturday, 28th September

This is the first day which I have no DMU activities or sports on. Thankfully, instead of rattling around my room for the day, I do have a few errands to run. After doing the weekly shop at the big Tesco, I also picked up a few extra things for Octavia’s party tonight. It’s the first time meeting up with Emily and Natalia since before leaving for Stockholm so I am euphorically looking forward to it. Of course, Saturday’s are reserved for football and with Nana’s VirginGo installed on my iPad, this makes it significantly easier rather than finding a dodgy stream that doesn’t buffer. Pleased with Middlesbrough’s two nil win over Stoke, I watched another film which had been on my watch list since its release: Miller’s Girl.

It was a low-budget flick, written by a writer with credits in the MCU and DC world but this was her first stand-alone film which she also directed. Ignoring the low ratings on IMDB, I thought it was enjoyable. Nothing too spectacular, but as a writer, I appreciate the quality vocabulary used in the film. Facilely, the story follows the life of a student (Jenna Ortega) who takes inspiration from her English Professor's book and uses it in her college thesis. The issue is that the Professor’s book contains an erotic scene and the student focuses entirely on that scene - writing fictitiously what could happen between the student and the professor. I actually enjoyed it though it may be because it is based around writing and the narrative using a varied lexicon instead of the mundane vernacular which appeals to the mass market. Martin Freeman and Jenna Ortega are personal favourites of mine too.

Moving onto the festivities this evening. I wasn’t told that it was Octavia’s birthday on Monday till very late into the evening. Honestly, when they were bringing out the cake, it should have been a giveaway but still I was ignorant of the fact. Natalia has continued working for the lecturer as a research assistant which she was with for the majority of her final year. She did think about doing a PhD but ultimately opted against it. Technically, she is taking a gap year to get more experience doing research as that is what she hopes to be doing in the future. As it is a paltry wage; she is still working at Starbucks on the weekend but as a manager now. Emily and Octavia work in the same care facility, helping children with learning difficulties through the medium of drama. Very noble indeed but it does suit their empathetic need to help people.

Natalia’s boyfriend was there: Mehmet, who coincidentally, I had already met at the futsal session yesterday. Small world huh. He is doing engineering and is part of the Racing Society which I’ve signed up to. I guess I’ll be seeing a lot of him. Good thing, he’s such a laid back type of person. Emily and Taves’ brothers also turned up; Mikey and Tobey who are good fun to be around. Mikey is 23 and works in a zoo. Toby is slightly more reserved but he is only 18 and starting UOL next week. I was the oldest in the room; a fact which I didn’t think bothered me but as I’ve had pointed out to me this week, this is starting to be a common occurrence. We played a lot of board games and a few party type games like charades. All good fun of course. Living in the building attached to Highcross is very convenient because it is a short walk through the shopping mall to get to their flat, however at two o’clock in the morning, I needed to add another fifteen minutes onto the journey, walking the circumference of the buildings.

Exhausted, I was in bed and asleep by three o’clock. My springy bed isn’t as big an issue as I initially thought. Accordingly, when you’re tired enough, you’ll sleep anywhere. After the busy week I’ve had, it’s not surprising that I haven’t had time to complain!

Friday, 27th September

After a busy four days meeting and greeting new people, today can be classed as a quasi day off. There is a ‘fun day’ at the DSU but based on the description of the event online, it sounds uninteresting and just a ploy in order to put another event on the schedule in order to make the purchase of the freshers wristbands seem good value. For the sole purpose of getting me out of the flat, I played football instead. As it was mid-afternoon, it was a much quieter session but the skill level was distinctly higher. This could be considered a training session for the DMU Futsal team because there were so many already distinguished players in the game. Nevertheless, I held my own. Scored a couple, assisted a few more. Try-outs will be interesting.

He shares similar views to me about Leicester. As he’s an Aussie, he only travels into Leicester for work but couldn’t live here. If it wasn’t for his wife, he would be moving back to Australia. He’s the first person I’ve met too who speaks complimentary about the British weather. After he was done, I had the rest of the day to myself. ‘Miss Sloane’ has been on my watchlist forever so I finally had the time to sit down and enjoy it. No football is on tonight so I just relaxed feeling ebullient about my successful freshers week.

Thursday, 26th September

Since the same societies were occupying the DSU building, I was going to miss the final day of freshers since I have already signed up for half a dozen societies and clubs. However, the gentle persuasion of Hannah changed my mind. I met up with her and her friend Zed and checked out the DSU. Due to the declining interest, a number of societies decided the third day wasn’t worth it so the tables were only occupying two floors, not three. Since almost everyone I’ve met this week is doing English Literature or some other form of Humanities, I have plenty in common with them. Zed is a facsimile of me from first year. Very taciturn and when she does speak, it’s laconic and meticulous. If she could have it her way, she would be getting dragged around freshers by Hannah. Anyway, afterwards we had a brief lunch from one of the stalls. It was very insipid food which is to be expected from makeshift food stalls. I bade farewell as I wanted to play football this afternoon and needed to get back to change.

All the sessions at the Watershed are still part of the ‘taster experience’ therefore are free and no need to book. This is perhaps a hindrance rather than a benefit when thirty people show up to play in a meagre sized hall. The lads present were the same few who persuaded me to join the trials so they were easy to talk to. Because of the vast interest, more than half the allotted time my team sat on the bench. Nevermind. Some of the current futsal team were there and they were scouting new talent.

After a quick shower and a brisk walk back to the Watershed, I partook in my second yoga session of the week. In the normal timetable, Yoga will clash with the DMU Hackers meetings on Thursday nights so I’m the epitome of ‘here for a good time, not a long time’ type thing. There was much more interest in this session which is understandable now everyone is finding their feet around campus. Francesca messaged me as the class was finishing saying she was running late. Poor time-management is clearly one of her faults as she missed the entirety of the session. This could be perceived as divine intervention because I was caught in a thunderstorm on the way home. After camping out in the Watershed for ten minutes, it showed no signs of passing. Bravely wrapping up, I ran back to St Martins House but I was rightly soaked through. I do hope I don’t get a cold.

Wednesday, 25th September

Before attending day two of the freshers fair, I picked up more essentials from town. Instead of doing one big shop, I’ve been doing it in stages. I also had another purpose for visiting town today: running trainers. I had been scouting the shops up north, but they were too dear, as Nana would say. Here, I have dedicated running shops and other independent retailers, which brought me an extended selection. The problem was, the prices skyrocketed in these retailers. Alas, I went to Sports Direct, even though I don’t like to support Mike Ashley. I managed to snag a pair of Asics for £64, which I think is reasonable. As long as I don’t go mad shopping in the next few weeks, I’ll be able to amortise the cost from the largesse of the parents.

The freshers fair was not as popular today compared to yesterday. The DSU was just as busy with societies, but the interest from the students had subsided. As promised, Dilan was there at the DMU Hackers table. We had a brief but interesting catch-up. He’s still at IBM until the end of the week, so it’s almost guaranteed that he’ll have a job after graduating. The Hackers meetings happen weekly, normally in Gateway House. Currently, that isn’t a problem, but during the second semester, I might have a seminar overlapping. Problem for later.

The most fortunate meeting of the day was seeing the stall for DMU Runners. This is a society that simply goes running together. Since I’ve just bought new Asics this morning, I thought it was prudent to have a chat with them. Coincidentally, they are meeting at the pub this evening to get to know the new intakes. More on this to come. Being circumspect, I said I’d think about it. That was because I had a previous engagement for this evening: yoga.

That’s right. I’m turning into a spiritual middle-aged man. I’ll be reading tea leaves and telling fortunes with a crystal ball if I’m not careful. Held at the Watershed, the session had only four others present. Strangely, all women; who’d have thought. The movements were slow and controlled. “It’s all about breathing out the bad energy and inhaling the good energy.” The session was an hour long, and we were doing all the classic poses: downward dog, the chair, deep lunge, the rag doll. Oh yeah, I’ve learnt a lot. We even meditated at the end; however, I admit I did find it difficult not to laugh during the silence at this new-age bollocks. “Focus on your breathing. Breathe in hopes and dreams. Breathe out negativity and impure thoughts.” What? Is this a front for a religious society now too?

I met Hannah and Francesca at this class. They’re starting their first year, both studying English Literature. Obviously, we had a lot of similar interests and have read almost identical books. Plenty of Dickens and Austen, F. Scott Fitzgerald, H.G. Wells, even Shakespeare. Who knows, there may even be hope yet of reciting Shakespeare monologues to each other.

After a quick shower and a bite to eat, I headed back into town to meet the runners at Soar Point. This is the go-to place for society socials because of the generous deals offered to students and also the large TV screens showing football. The Karting society has an arrangement with the staff to watch the F1 there on Sundays too, as long as it doesn’t clash with the football. No doubt, I’ll be a regular if all goes well.

The Running Society is good fun. There is a good mix of people, boys and girls. Very affable. We played cards and Uno, spoke about our subjects, and shared our hobbies other than running. I was at the head of the table with the committee members: Katie and Lauren, who were the undersecretaries. Tobias, who had graduated last year, is a very funny man and also a paramedic, so he’s a handy friend to have. I was in the Soar till around eleven before we headed out to Firebug, another pub whose USP was offering a retro gaming experience whilst people drink. Mario Karts, Black Ops zombies, MW3. I felt a wave of nostalgia just seeing the consoles. It was just myself and the committee members who ventured to Firebug, as the rest took the long voyage to Black Cats, which was part of the Freshers experience. As a non-drinker, I haven’t invested the £40 in a wristband to get me access to these clubs, but I was content to stay with the runners. I heard the Running Society is well-run. Well run—see what I did there? They have a group running almost every night of the week, so we can pick and choose our days and distances. They also do social runs where they dress up, which will be … eventful.

Tuesday, 24th September

Mark the date. My first attempt at being sociable. The DSU building was packed from the ground floor to the top with societies and sports teams, all trying to persuade you to join. No wonder I opted to avoid this in past years. This would have been an introvert's worst nightmare, having everyone asking questions and engaging in conversation. Now, however, I welcomed the challenge. After queuing for the best part of an hour to even get in, it was actually enjoyable because I had previously looked online at potential simpatico societies. The stalls I was actively looking for included: DMU Hackers (a computer science society), Karters (a go-karting club that holds socials where they watch F1 together), and Demon Media (the De Montfort magazine and radio proprietors). I thought this triumvirate would be a good place to start.

Having scoured from top to bottom, I found the latter two but no sign of DMU Hackers. Serendipitously, I ran into Dilan on my way home, who happens to be on the committee. The reason for their absence was due to a lack of space in the building, but they would be there tomorrow.

The Karters were a passionate bunch, and they all shared simpatico views on F1 as I did. This looks promising. I am less enthusiastic about Demon Media because the quality of the magazine looks mediocre. In conjunction with the poor design and large blank spaces permeating each page, the writing was subpar. I probably sound like Liam when I say that I could do a much more comprehensive job if I were to take over. Similarly to the futsal, I’m not looking for a heavy workload, just a medium in which I can publish a few articles now and again. The first meetings are happening next week, so I will definitely give them a go at least. On my way out, the Futsal society cornered me, and they didn’t let me bypass them without signing up for the trials happening in the next few weeks. It’s nice to be wanted.

Back at St Martins House, a FIFA tournament was meant to be held, but the staff couldn’t work the TV, so that has been postponed till next week. Nevertheless, I held residence in the common room with a few others, enjoying the conviviality. There were Nigerians, Londoners, Brummies, and me—purposely confusing them all with my northern vernacular. “I canny see the point in gandering about for no reason like” was a personal favourite malapropism of mine. The look of bewilderment on their faces was amusing in itself. Anyway, I have gained a few more followers on Insta, so we can keep in touch. Overall, they seem like good people.

Monday, 23rd September

The first week of moving into new accommodation is always the most expensive. Stocking up on the essentials plus the weekly shop too, normally brings me to around £50 for the week. I was exhausted for some reason yesterday so I’ve been putting off the shopping and living on bread and the frozen foods which the local Tesco Express stocks. I was carrying out the menial tasks today such as updating my student card and re-registering for the forthcoming year so I was active anyway. The Diamond Jubilee Leisure centre is on the way to the big Tesco’s so I enquired about the free classes available to me. Unbeknownst to me, it was completely the wrong place, leaving the receptionist perplexed about my questions. Turns out, it’s actually the Watershed where the DMU Active session takes place - a DMU owned complex which has dedicated staff which also houses many of the sports societies too.

Following my productive conversation with the staff, I received a sheet with all the taster sessions on which would be happening throughout freshers. As futsal is the most popular, they are holding four sessions this week so everyone has a chance. No need to sign up or register. Just turn up and play. Simple. The futsal session was eventful. I wanted something relaxed, (if there is such a thing in football), to keep me active throughout the year. Evidently there must have been a few members of the DMU futsal squad there scouting because I’ve been ushered to go to the try-outs. I didn’t particularly think my performance was as good as it normally is but the enthusiasm which the players had when speaking about my skill was too ingratiating for me to say no. I guess I’ll be playing competitive futsal this year…

My third flatmate moved in today too - Esther. She’s studying microbiology at UOL so I’m not sure how much I’ll see of her. She seems congenial like Summer but I still don’t know how close we are all going to be.
