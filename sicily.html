Workaway - Sicily

Part Twenty Two - Planes, Trains & Automobiles

Monday, 10th February 18:30 GMT

Back on home turf after six hours travelling. The flight was the longest and yet felt the shortest. Orson Welles kept me occupied throughout. The bus driver taking me to Mile End from Stansted clearly loathed his position. Very irascible, it didn’t take much for him to make it known that he was only the bus driver and not the tour guide for visiting tourists.

There were a few Italians with me on the bus who I recognised from the train. The communication barrier proved to be an issue when it came to tickets, but rather than attempt a universal gesture or the time-honoured art of pointing, the driver opted for the classic British approach—repeating the same sentence at an increasingly deafening volume, as if English miraculously becomes comprehensible when shouted.

Meanwhile, back in Palermo, Silvio will be welcoming his next batch of guests—three French girls eager to explore Sicily in its entirety. They’ve chosen Palermo as their starting point, a logical decision on paper, though the reality of beginning their dream holiday in a crumbling slum may temper their enthusiasm. According to Silvio, the rest of Sicily is far more beautiful. I sincerely hope, for their sake, that he’s right.

I do wonder how Silvio will adapt to their presence. Will he restrain his more egregious sexist diatribes, or will he take this as an opportunity to really double down? My money’s on the latter. I’ve had a hands-on week, putting in long hours sanding, varnishing, building furniture, and installing shelves. When he asked what skills the girls could bring to the table, they proudly announced: “We can babysit and cook meals.” I’m genuinely surprised he let them stay, given their complete lack of relevant abilities. But, then again, that’s no longer my concern. I’m out. No more risk of getting mugged the second I step outside.

And yet, sat in my student accommodation, looking at the concrete jungle out of my window, I miss not being in Palermo. Despite everything. I would still pick Palermo over Leicester. Not just because of the weather, or the people. But because it’s got character. It was chaotic, unpredictable, alive—a welcome break from the monotony.

Now, reality reasserts itself. Tomorrow, my next module begins. I have an internship interview in the morning, Demon Magazine articles to write, FYP meetings with my supervisor, and Leicester Comedy Festival shows to cover for Demon FM.

Four more months, then I can finally say goodbye to this city—a place that functions less like a home and more like an incessant black hole of ennui.

Part Twenty One - Reflections

Monday, 10th February 10:35 CET

Palmero airport security is lackadaisical at best. No staff yelling diatribes at us; reminding us the that liquids are prohibited and electricals must be visible. No staff of any kind in fact. Even when we’re going through the metal detectors; there are adjoining rooms between each x-ray presumably filled with SWAT in case were smuggling more than a litre of water through. If this isn’t the case, it’s because it’s a mafia airport and smuggling is so omnipresent, the staff have collectively resigned - or are susceptible to venality or depravities.

Now it’s time to reflect on yet another workaway volunteering experience. Was it the week I was expecting – FUCK NO. Would I go back and change it; also no! I was expecting to be hosted at a cosy little villa on the periphery of Palermo. Maybe a little closer to the beach, a short train ride away from the city centre. This expectation was almost correct. What I didn’t expect whilst staying there was to be in the heart of mafia controlled territory, targeted to be mugged and staying with a man who’s views on democracy are similar to those of Karl Marx.

Silvio… is a character, I guess if I had to sum him up in a sentence. He’s sixty year old and though he's had a relatively successful life; something tells me it wasn’t without crossing paths with the law a few times. Highlights: hosting an illegal hostel, leader of an anti-vax group in Sicily, building his own residence on a vacant spot of land with only the permission of the landowner and not the authorities… need I go on.

Many conversations with Silvio spiralled into impassioned tirades about the oppression the proletariat must endure under the iron grip of government control. He’s a zealot—one whose inferences rarely follow a logical trajectory, instead hurtling towards wild non sequiturs with unshakable conviction. He is, in no uncertain terms, sexist, homophobic, implacable, parsimonious, and utterly unrepentant. Steadfast in his beliefs, no matter how contrarian or inflammatory, he clings to them with the fervour of a man who has never once considered he might be wrong.

And yet, despite—or perhaps because of—all this, my week with Silvio has been one of the most fascinating of my life.

I was ostensibly a guest in order to help Silvio with his woodworking portfolio, restoring furniture, hanging shelves, building units for the guest house. Capable work for an artisan like myself. Silvio was amenable for what hours and days I worked; allowing me to make the most of the days in the sun.

Because of the prosaic surroundings of Tommaso Natale, getting the train into Palermo became the status quo. Beating the system and avoiding the ticket officers, I fully embraced the criminal pedigree one requires to live in Sicily.

Now, the citadel. I was picturing The White Lotus. Instead I got Goodfellas and Heretic. On the walking tour, it was explained that The White Lotus wasn’t even filmed here. Blasphemy! Godfather Part III, however was filmed on the stairs of the Palermo Opera House. Many years ago, Al Pacino was shot on those very stairs.

On each visit to the capital of Sicily, I walked a different direction, accumulating over fifty miles. Not all districts were of the idyllic quality that I was expecting but that is a given for every city in the world.

It's hard to comprehend the amount of revelations that have happened this week.

At times, it felt like I was wandering through the slums of Yemen, no laws, no respect. I’ve been in witness to a traffic collision, walked straight through Mafia territory brandishing my camera for all to see, got targeted for a mugging, had a car accident, spent week cohabiting with a conspiracy theorist, helped build a church for conversion therapy, showered in sewer water and had more conversations in Spanish than in Italian.

And yet, I’ve had some of the best meals of my life. Palermo’s chaotic, unfiltered, and bizarrely captivating in its own crumbling grandeur and if I’ve learnt anything in this eventful week, it’s never trust a pretty girl with sticky fingers.

Part Twenty - Looney Tune Repairs

Sunday, 9th February 22:20 CET

The relentless downpour has us effectively under house arrest. With nowhere to go and nothing to do, I resign myself to the dubious pleasure of watching Silvio engage in his peculiar brand of DIY. There are odd jobs to be done, and though he’s happy to tackle them alone, he occasionally summons me when an extra pair of hands is required.

First, he’s welding a wooden foot back onto the underside of the couch. I ask, more out of morbid curiosity than genuine interest, “Why are you welding it?” His response is as baffling as it is final: “The glue isn’t strong enough.” I stare at him, then at the wooden leg, which is already beginning to bow under the heat. It’s wood, it’ll just snap. But I’m out of here tomorrow, so frankly, it’s not my problem. I retreat to my room to block him out, but the flickering lights make that impossible.

Stepping back into the living room, I find that he’s wired up his welder using an extension cord with exposed wiring—just loose, frayed copper strands sitting there, waiting for someone (likely me) to step on them and be electrocuted. Carefully stepping over the trap, I go to get some water. When I turn back round, Silvio is sprawled on the floor, a screwdriver jammed into an electrical socket.

I don’t ask. I don’t want to know. Every instinct in my body screams to tell him to turn off the power before plunging metal objects into live outlets, but I know better by now. Health and safety is, after all, a British concept, and Silvio operates on an entirely different wavelength. “The World’s not a dangerous place Harry”. I’ll escribe it onto his tombstone.

Half time of the Villa vs Spurs game, he’s perched atop a ladder, half naked. Top half thankfully, didn’t want to see his Italian Pride on my last night in Sicily. This trip’s been memorable enough. He's precariously balanced on the kitchen table. Apparently, one of the bulbs isn’t bright enough, though I suspect it’s not the light that’s not bright. I watch him teeter for a moment, bracing for the inevitable crash, but decide I don’t want to be privy to whatever impending disaster is about to unfold. I return to my room, feigning ignorance.

I wish he did enlighten me to his itinerary because we could have got the job done – correctly. There’s the right way of doing things and then Silvio’s way of doing things. Every task he completes, normally generate three more.

It’s like watching someone attempt DIY based solely on Looney Tunes logic. Frankly, it’s a miracle he’s managed to construct this bungalow at all. Apart from the dubious wiring, malfunctioning plumbing, erratic heating, blocked sewage pipes and door locks that are completely arbitrary, it’s a canny little place.

Chicken & roast potatoes for tea tonight. We talked about family and relationships. He gushed over his daughter; Silvia – daughter of Silvio. Couldn’t get much more Sicilian if they tried. He had nothing but good words to say about her. Unfortunately, given his sexist proclivities, the good epithets he used were similar to those that Donald Trump would use. “Very good facial features, and lumpy breasted.” Lumpy breasted? That’s a sign of cancer is it not?

I make my excuses using the pretence that I must pack, which isn’t technically a lie. Even so, I’m ready to leave. One more night then I’m back on home turf, where I don’t have a target on my back or the Mafia lurking in the shadows.

Part Nineteen - More People Die in Car Accidents than Mafia Shootings

Sunday, 9th February 14:39 CET

Palermo's only a twenty-minute drive, but I have never been so hyper-aware of my surroundings.

It’s like the hazard perception test on steroids. Pedestrians meander into the middle of the road with total disregard for oncoming traffic, cars lurch out of junctions without warning, and mopeds zip between lanes like they have a death wish. Pure, unfiltered chaos.

To make matters worse, Silvio’s clapped-out shitbox is barely holding itself together. I never truly understood what the Top Gear lads meant when they said they’d “lost third gear” until now. Except in my case, I’d lost second, fourth, and fifth. Where they went, I have no idea, but I was left revving the hell out of first gear before catapulting straight into third. Maybe that’s why nobody here stops for anything—because they’ve only got two functioning gears to work with.

By some miracle, I make it to Palermo in one piece.

Parking is not an issue at all. We can park anywhere – literally. Double stacking, sure. Pull up on the pavement, that’s fine. I have seen one driver park up at a traffic light and then just walk off. So, I followed suit, blocking someone in an alleyway. Hopefully, I don’t come back and the car’s been stolen or clamped; or bricked.

The walking tour didn’t disappoint though I had visited all the stops along the route sometime over the past week. Knowing a little history of the place is always a nice addition to any trip. This tour covered everything from the municipal buildings, the history of Sicilian monarchy to the Mafia and tourist stereotypes.

One every walking tour I’ve attended, there are always the same personality types drawn to them.

First, there’s the wannabe-teacher—usually a middle-aged man, exuding the energy of someone who once taught history at a secondary school and never quite got over it. He interrupts the guide with unnecessary corrections, obscure facts, and “actually” statements that no one asked for. The guide mentions Italian fascist in the 40s? Well, Wannabe-Teacher read a book on the subject once, and now we all have to hear about it.

We have the Instagram Couple always lagging behind, trying to get the perfect shot while the guide explains something important. The guy’s in charge of angles, the girl’s adjusting her hair every five seconds, and at half the tour is spent waiting for them to catch up. Bonus points if one of them is wearing a ridiculously impractical outfit, like stilettos on cobblestone streets like the girl in Vienna.

Then there’s the silent lurker. This one’s a mystery. No questions, no reactions, just a faint nod every now and then. Is he enjoying himself? Who knows. Is he secretly a journalist researching an exposé on walking tours? Possibly. He’s either absorbing every word with monk-like intensity or mentally composing a list of ways this could all be more efficient. Either way, he’ll disappear immediately after the tour ends without so much as a goodbye.

There’s always a lost tourist who invariably makes their way onto the tour. They look perpetually confused, checking their phone for where they actually meant to be while politely pretending to care about 17th-century architecture. Just following the crowd, hoping we return to the metropolitan part of the city. In Copenhagen, a woman joined us just as the tour was ending and resulted in giving a tip after been cornered by the tour guide.

And lastly, there’s me; half in it for the history, half just looking for an excuse to walk to make sure I cover all the tourist attractions. Occasionally, I’ll ask a question to prove I’m engaged, but really, I’m just here for the photographs.

I still have half a day’s work to do so I’m driving back to Tommaso Natale around 2ish. Weaving through the traffic, the inevitable happens—someone rear-ends me. Technically, it’s my fault but it wouldn’t be if we were in England. I stopped at a zebra crossing to let people cross but of course this isn’t the normal thing to do here. The car behind doesn’t stop, hitting me. The guy pulls up next to me and probably delivers a polemic in Italian and then drives off. No details exchanges, insurance policies dug out the glove compartment. Off we go.

Now comes the real dilemma: do I tell Silvio? Considering he wasn’t remotely bothered about our scrape the other day; I’m deciding to embrace his laissez-faire attitude and keep it to myself. I’ll be gone this time tomorrow so I just need to hold my nerve till them.

Part Eighteen - One Last Time

Sunday, 9th February 08:15 CET

Since I’m persona non grata in Tommaso Natale without the risk of being robbed and I can’t sit around indulging Silvio with his conspiracy theories, I’m heading into Palermo for my last outing – probably ever. Not because I’m going to get murdered but because once I leave Sicily, I don’t see a reason to return.

Every time I arrived at a new destination, I oblige in my normal routine of booking a walking tour. This morning is the first day where I’ve been awake early enough to be in Palermo by 10am. Taking Sally’s warning seriously, I asked Silvio for a lift to the station and received the expected apathy followed by dismissal.

Because he still wants to be accommodating (and because I get to rate him upon the conclusion of my trip) but not an accessory to hysteria, he tells me to take his car and just drive to Palermo. I’m so excited. Hopefully, I don’t collide with any fences - or cyclists. Though, if you could see the condition of the battered Yaris, one more dent wouldn’t make a difference.

Part Seventeen - Embracing Fate

Saturday, 8th February 19:20 CET

Desperate for an escape, I fled back to Palermo. Anything to avoid being held captive by Silvio’s latest conspiracy. His brand of paranoia is exhausting, an endless monologue of half-baked theories that threaten to erode my sanity. Sally gave me a lift on his way home, taking the opportunity to hammer his point once more: take this seriously. Ignore Silvio’s laissez-faire attitude—his detachment isn’t wisdom, it’s just his modus operandi. (Just the way he is).

Part of me still suspects this is all theatrical fanfare, an elaborate ghost story for the unwitting tourist. Until I see a mafia-sanctioned abduction in broad daylight, or a restaurant engulfed in flames, I’ll remain cautiously incredulous.

At least Palermo offered another fascinating excursion. I stuck to the northeast, weaving through the docks where the city meets the sea. The sun was warm, the salty breeze carried the scent of fish and diesel, and boats bobbed lazily among the waves. For a moment, Palermo felt almost serene—until the screech of brakes shattered the illusion. A rickshaw on its side, wheels still spinning in the air. An elderly couple standing dazed nearby. I didn’t linger; the growing crowd blocked any view of the aftermath. I’m honestly surprised, I’ve not seen more accidents in this past week.

Meanwhile, I’ve grown accustomed to crossing these streets, though it’s still unnerving to have to walk into traffic and pray that drivers will, at the very least, hesitate. A stark reminder to remain aware of their eyelines—if they don’t see you, you don’t exist.

After a long afternoon wandering through Palermo’s tangled streets and devouring another few chapters of Welles’ absurdly cinematic life, I’m back on the train. I text Sally to collect me from Cardillo station. Silvio, predictably, thinks we’re being hysterical—that I’m just another subservient cog in the great machine of fear, manipulated by big organisations and their reign of terror.

Since it’s likely my last outing to the city since I’ve got one day of work left; I’ll miss visiting one quarter of it. There’s a rawness to it, something both grand and dilapidated, beautiful in its own imperfect way. I’m still trying to find the picture-perfect holiday destination that I see depicted on TV.

Part Sixteen - Disconcerted? A Little

Saturday, 8th February 13:35 CET

We continued our efforts in restoring the furniture that’s building up in the church. I’m doing the menial jobs such as sanding and varnishing. Repeating the same continuous motion becomes relaxing after a while. Almost tranquil and peaceful. Then it had to start raining so the second half of my work detail continued indoors. I’ve been reorganising Silvio’s Shakespeare folios into comedies, tragedies and histories. They’re in Italian but I can deduce which play is which by the title and picture on the front.

Bet you didn’t know that Shakespeare was merely a public figurehead, while his secret lover—conveniently erased from history—was the true genius behind the plays. At least, that’s the latest gem Silvio has decided to enlighten me with. Now, it’s hardly scandalous to suggest that Shakespeare may have been gay, but to reduce him to some illiterate puppet, a glorified Elizabethan influencer? The sheer ignominy of it! My innate British heritage is screaming; I think I’ve given myself an ulcer from biting my tongue so frequently. How dare he disparage the Bard with such unqualified drivel?

Sally walks in, interrupting Silvio’s conspiracy lessons, desperation in his voice.

He explains, “I’ve been seen”.

A newcomer in the neighbourhood, carrying an expensive-looking camera—an unmistakable red flag. I suppose it was that two-and-a-half hour walk from Tommaso Natale to Palermo centre that put me on the radar. Walking through every neighbourhood, snapping away. I guess that was asinine given the dodgy areas I was wandering through.

So, in no uncertain terms, I’m ordered not to leave the compound; get Silvio to drive me to the station and text whenever I return so that either he or Sally can collect me. And if I dare to wander around Tommaso Natale, I must ensure it’s glaringly obvious that I’m not carrying a backpack.

Silvio, of course, waves off the warning with his usual insouciance. The world isn’t dangerous, remember? Perhaps to him, with his boundless optimism and questionable past, but I’ve always been one to err on the side of caution. Do I believe Sally’s concerns? I’m unsure. Part of me thinks it’s all performative theatrics, a melodrama put on for the benefit of an impressionable outsider. Yet, another part—one that leans towards self-preservation—knows better than to dismiss the unease outright.

Maybe I need something concrete, a sign that I’m truly in the crosshairs. A Memento Mori delivered to my doorstep, a Sicilian calling card that screams: You should have listened.

Part Fifteen - The Man Beneath the Façade

Friday, 7th February 22:11 CET

Silvio is NUTS. Like, genuinely, certifiably nuts. I had my suspicions early on, little clues dropped in past conversations, but it’s taken me a while to fully piece together just how deep this madness runs. He’s like Dominic, but with a more unhinged collection of conspiracy theories rattling around in his brain.

Before COVID, he was a music teacher. That is, until he refused to get the vaccine, putting both students and staff at risk. Not content with just being a personal health hazard, he took it upon himself to start proselytising, urging others to resist the jab as well. Naturally, he got sacked. Rather than accept that actions have consequences, he doubled down, convinced that he was the last bastion of truth in a world brainwashed by “Big Pharma” and government control.

After that, his response was to open his home to complete strangers, all to prove that the world isn’t a big and scary place. I, for one, completely disagree. The world is a big and scary place, and people like Silvio are a prime example of why. I’m not saying don’t explore; I’m saying remain vigilant and assess potential risks. But, of course, arguing with him would be like reasoning with a lunatic, so I nod along and let him ramble.

Then there’s his newfound adoration for Communism, which, hilariously, seems to have stemmed from a single Workaway volunteer from Poland. This guy lived under Communist rule and, apparently, now makes less money in a democratic society than he did under an authoritarian regime. Instead of recognising that as anecdotal at best, Silvio has taken this as indisputable proof that communism is the superior way of life. Never mind the historical evidence, the oppression, or the fact that people literally risked their lives escaping it—no, one broke Polish bloke is all the confirmation Silvio needs.

And then we get to the real kicker: his entire living situation is dodgy as hell. He built this house from the ground up not out of some noble, off-the-grid, self-sufficient dream, but because he was banned from owning property after running an illegal hostel at his previous residence. Again, this isn’t speculation—this is coming directly from him, said with the casual air of someone discussing the weather. He wasn’t confessing, he was bragging.

But the cherry on top? The church. The derelict church he’s so lovingly restoring isn’t just some quaint pet project or an attempt to give back to the community. No, no, no. He’s renovating it because his son is gay, and he genuinely believes this will somehow “bring him back to the light.” I mean, is it just a church, or is Silvio planning something more grandiose, some twisted little conversion therapy cult under the guise of religious salvation? At this point, I wouldn’t put anything past him.

It’s actually mental. The more I learn, the more I’m grateful I’m only here for the week instead of a month.

Part Fourteen - Palmero = Done ✅

Friday, 7th February 19:02 CET

Silvio and I decided to take another day off since the weather was perfect. After he prepared me yet another sublime sandwich, I set off for Palermo for the fifth day in a row. But today was different—I walked the entire way. Two and a half hours, passing every station along the train line.

Once I reached the city centre, I didn’t stop there. I continued south, hugging the coastline, hoping to stumble upon some natural beauty—golden sands, crystal-clear waters, something to redeem Palermo’s rugged exterior. But as I walked further, it became clear that if I kept going in search of paradise, I might as well circumnavigate the entire island. Eventually, I turned back, accepting that Palermo is not quite the grand holiday destination I had imagined. I spent the rest of the afternoon reading Orson Welles’ biography in the park.

After five days of exploring and covering nearly nine miles a day, I feel I know this city like the back of my hand. Each quarter has its own distinct character. The northeast is picturesque, tourist-friendly, and home to the harbour. The northwest blends verdant parks and grand cathedrals with creeping signs of poverty. The southeast is a grittier stretch—mainly an exit road flanked by a bleak, debris-strewn coastline. The southwest, meanwhile, could easily be mistaken for a slum in the Middle East.

Still, I’ve seen it all.

Part Thirteen - Screams, Scams and Skinny Jeans

Thursday, 6th February 23:15 CET

My first real-world experience with the perfidy of the Sicilians happened tonight. I’d heard the stories about the Mafia and witnessed firsthand the poor conditions they impose on the capital, but a part of me still found it hard to believe the veracity of the tales I’d been told.

Let me start from the beginning. After a very filling tea consisting of - you guessed it, dough, potatoes and ham in a new combination - Sally and I drove to pub around fifteen minutes away from Silvio’s place to watch the football. He’s still slightly depressed after his beloved Arsenal were humiliated last night. He wanted to see Spurs receive the same treatment but that didn’t come to fruition.

As mentioned, he is Sri Lankan but has lived here in Sicily for over a decade working as a handyman. He came over on a cricket scholarship and never left, very much suiting the congruous criminality of Tommaso Natale.

The pub wasn’t in Palermo but in a place called Vespi. It’s authentically Sicilian. Tourists don’t come this far out of the city - for safety reasons. Everyone’s drinking scotch, smoking cigars. I can’t even see the ceiling because of the thick layer of smoke. We were at the bar enjoying the game, staying out of trouble, until half time at least.

Then we hear a scream from outside. The only reason I mention it is because of the slightly obscure reaction of everyone in the bar. The reaction was no reaction. It sounded different from a girl just falling over. To me, it sounded more like a shriek of pain but I am just speculating.

I instinctively get off my bar stool to go look at the commotion, but my arm’s seized by Sally who hasn’t moved. I’m trying to remember but I don’t think anyone was too perturbed by the scream. It was definitely loud enough that everyone could hear. I get back on my stool and ask Sally what’s going on. He’s swirling his scotch around the glass and seems to be lost in a reverie.

The game finishes and Sally’s chatting to friends at the bar. This is one of the only sports bars outside of Palermo that’s not Mafia controlled - apparently – which is why we’re here. I’m still not entirely sure what that actually means. Are Mafia bars like fronts for laundering money? Are they storing stolen goods? Or just skimming a cut of the profits under the guise of “protection” fees? Of course, all my conclusions are based on A Bronx Tale, Goodfellas, and Donnie Brasco.

I’m waiting for Sally outside for a few minutes. It’s a convivial atmosphere. It’s a young crowd. My sort of age. Out of nowhere, an Italian bird comes up to me and starts a conversation. I don’t speak Italian, and she doesn’t speak English, though I do speak a little Spanish. Va va vroom.

With me being a cheeky-chappie, and my “everyone loves an Englishman” attitude, we’re fumbling through this conversation. Her attempts to bridge the language barrier with her gestures and eager smiles are more endearing than effective, but we’re getting somewhere.

She’s clearly growing impatient, though. With a flirtatious twirl of her hand, she leans in closer. Before I even have time to register what’s happening, she swings both arms around me and is kissing me. At first, I’m caught off guard, thinking this is just the Italian way—passionate, spontaneous, a trademark of Sicilian culture. It’s all part of the dance, right? The grand romantic gesture, the universal language of love that everyone talks about when describing the Italian spirit.

Just as I’m patting my ego on the back - her hands move lower. I feel a sudden, unexpected tug at the pocket of my jeans. The subtle motion is so fluid, it could have been mistaken for just another affectionate touch—if I hadn’t felt the unmistakable shift in weight as she tried to lift my phone from my pocket.

I’m being robbed.

It was an odd feeling. For a second, I’m frozen. My brain is registering what’s happening, but my body lags behind, unsure. As soon as I felt significant weight shift, I think instinct kicked in.

Thank God for my skinny jeans. I’ve never been so grateful for tight pockets in my life. With a quick movement, I catch my phone, her supple fingers let go and I’m just looking her directly in the eyes.

This creeped me out. I’ve never seen anyone’s eyes change so vehemently before. Especially someone with such beautiful baby blues… She went from Holly Golightly to Baby Jane real fast. And we all know how terrified I am of the latter.

Before anything is said, Sally’s once again grabbing me by the arm leading me toward the car. Now I get it. The romance, the flirting? All part of the game. It’s not my charm she’s interested in—just what’s in my trousers. My phone, I mean. My phone.

I was experiencing a bit of a head rush as we were driving off. I semi-knew what happened but I was still fuzzy. In the car, Sally’s letting me in on the street scams. “They work in teams,” he said, “and if they know you’ve got expensive gear, they’ll target you again.” Well, great to know, and here I thought she was just entranced by my English charm. I’ll have to remember this scam for when the Parisian lucky lucky men come my way.

I’m still confused about the scream though. He didn’t answer that question, but I doubt it’s as sinister as what we’re all thinking. Sicily may not be as progressive as I had thought but it’s not ran by gangsters and the Mafia in the way that the movies will have us believe. Or am I still being incredibly naïve?

Part Twelve - Palermo at My Own Pace

Thursday, 6th February 18:50 CET

My first day off, and after my little tribulation last night, I needed it. The sun was out, the tasks weren’t urgent, and Silvio let me have the full day to myself—on the condition that I’d work one day over the weekend. No complaints from me, since the weather’s set to turn miserable by Saturday night anyway.

So, back to Palermo. I tried to catch a walking tour at 11 AM, only to find they don’t exist. Just the 10 AM ones, which was annoying. But since I’ve already combed through this city—top to bottom, east to west, seeing both the picturesque and the profane, the sublime among the malign—today was a much more relaxed affair. Stuck to the high street, indulged in a little window shopping, sat down in every park I passed, and gave my feet the break they desperately deserved.

For lunch, Silvio had made me a sandwich. Not very Italian I know, but regardless, it was one of the most delicious sandwiches I’ve ever had in my life. It puts my turkey and cheese ensemble to shame. It was the size of a small pancake, filled with fried chicken, lettuce, mozzarella, mayonnaise, zucchini, aubergine, a rare herb found only in the Himalayas, the blood of a mountain goat, the vestiges of the grain forged in the heart of Mount Vesuvius. You get the picture.

After finishing one fiction yesterday, I started the Orson Welles biography right in front of the Norman Palace.
